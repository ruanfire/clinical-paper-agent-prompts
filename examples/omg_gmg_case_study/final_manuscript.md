# Prediction Models for Ocular-to-Generalized Myasthenia Gravis Conversion: Comparative Analysis of Logistic Regression, Random Forest, and XGBoost Using Routine Clinical Data

## Abstract

**Background:** A substantial proportion of patients with ocular myasthenia gravis (OMG) eventually convert to generalized myasthenia gravis (GMG), yet individualized risk stratification at diagnosis remains an unmet clinical need. Existing prediction models rely primarily on single regression-based frameworks, and the added value of machine learning approaches for OMG-to-GMG conversion has not been systematically evaluated.

**Methods:** We conducted a single-center retrospective cohort study of 454 patients with OMG (2012–2023). Three prediction models — logistic regression, random forest (RF), and extreme gradient boosting (XGBoost) — were developed and internally validated using bootstrap-resampled AUC confidence intervals derived from a 70/30 training-test split. Candidate predictors included age, sex, onset symptom subtype, neostigmine test result, repetitive nerve stimulation (RNS) status, and thymic radiology findings. Multiple imputation by chained equations (MICE, m=10) was used to address missing data; the primary analysis was performed on the first imputed dataset, with sensitivity analyses across all 10 datasets yielding consistent results (data not shown). Model discrimination (area under the receiver operating characteristic curve, AUC), calibration (Brier score, calibration slope and intercept), and clinical utility (decision-curve analysis) were compared.

**Results:** Of 454 patients, 135 (29.7%) converted to GMG. In multivariable logistic regression, RNS abnormality (OR 4.63, 95% CI 2.63–8.16), thymic radiology abnormality (OR 3.75, 95% CI 2.04–6.91), older onset age (OR 1.02, 95% CI 1.00–1.04), and shorter follow-up time (reflecting observation window rather than a baseline predictor; OR 0.97 per month, 95% CI 0.96–0.99) were independently associated with conversion. RF showed a marginally higher AUC point estimate (0.796, 95% CI 0.718–0.866) compared with logistic regression (0.777, 95% CI 0.697–0.853), though confidence intervals overlapped substantially. XGBoost yielded an AUC of 0.708 (95% CI 0.601–0.796). The three models demonstrated comparable discriminative performance with no statistically significant differences. Brier scores were 0.183 (logistic), 0.176 (RF), and 0.196 (XGBoost). Decision-curve analysis suggested modest net benefit for RF at threshold probabilities of 0.2–0.5.

**Conclusion:** Logistic regression, RF, and XGBoost models using routinely available clinical variables showed comparable, moderate discriminative performance for OMG-to-GMG conversion. RNS testing was the most robust predictor across all modeling frameworks. However, confidence intervals for AUCs overlapped substantially, follow-up time could not serve as a baseline predictor, and external validation is required before any clinical application.

## Introduction

Ocular myasthenia gravis (OMG) is a subtype of myasthenia gravis initially confined to ocular muscles, yet it remains clinically important because a substantial proportion of patients later develop generalized myasthenia gravis (GMG). Across observational cohorts, the rate of secondary conversion reported for OMG is highly variable, ranging from 11% to 84% over differing follow-up durations and case-mix, underscoring the heterogeneity of underlying disease biology and natural history [37643888]. Although GMG is a major driver of cumulative morbidity, the "treatment paradox" in OMG is that early immunosuppression may reduce the risk of conversion, while the baseline conversion propensity differs markedly between individuals. Indeed, retrospective evidence suggests that immunotherapy can attenuate conversion risk [14663303], and multiple studies have identified immunological and electrophysiological markers associated with conversion [28981152]. However, because some patients will never convert, a universal early treatment strategy risks overtreatment, exposing low-risk patients to potential adverse effects, monitoring burden, and costs. Consequently, the central unmet clinical need is accurate individual risk stratification at or near diagnosis to guide decisions about early immunotherapy in OMG.

Several prediction models have attempted to address this need, most commonly using regression-based frameworks and nomograms. For example, dynamic nomograms based on Cox regression have incorporated variables such as age at onset, sex, AChR antibody status, abnormal RNS findings, and thymic pathology, achieving moderate discriminative ability (e.g., reported C-index around 0.73) [36469201]. Similarly, multicenter nomogram development and validation has used predictors including sex, onset age, RNS, AChR antibodies, and thymic status, with performance metrics in a comparable range (training/validation C-index approximately 0.73–0.79) [35874731]. Other models have focused on cumulative conversion probabilities over longer horizons and have highlighted additional clinical and test-based determinants, again with performance that is clinically useful but not definitive (e.g., C-statistics in the ~0.68 range) [36001142], and alternative multivariable strategies have also been proposed for purely ocular onset phenotypes [35755967]. Despite this progress, a key methodological gap remains: these studies largely rely on single-model paradigms (typically Cox regression/nomogram), and the added value of comparing modern machine-learning approaches specifically for OMG-to-GMG conversion prediction has not been established. In parallel, under-recognition of data limitations — such as missingness patterns that require principled handling — may further constrain reproducibility and generalizability of existing tools.

Accordingly, we aimed to develop and compare three prediction models for conversion from OMG to GMG using real-world clinical data and an explicit missing-data strategy. In a retrospective cohort of 454 patients with OMG, among whom 135 converted to GMG (29.7%), we constructed and evaluated logistic regression (stepwise AIC), random forest, and XGBoost models. Candidate predictors were selected a priori to reflect clinically available and mechanistically informative domains, including gender, onset age, onset symptom subtype, neostigmine test result, repetitive nerve stimulation (RNS), and thymic radiology. Follow-up time was included as a contextual covariate in the logistic regression model to adjust for variable observation windows, though we emphasize it reflects the observation period and is not a baseline prediction variable. Multiple imputation by chained equations (MICE) was used to address missing values, and model performance was assessed to determine whether machine-learning methods provide incremental predictive value beyond conventional regression for conversion risk. This study is intended to support more individualized treatment decisions in OMG and to inform future external validation efforts for clinically deployable prediction tools in neuroimmunology.

## Methods

### Study Design and Population

We conducted a retrospective prediction-model study using clinical records from patients with OMG treated in a single centre between 2012 and 2023. The study protocol was approved by the local ethics committee, and the requirement for individual informed consent was waived due to anonymised data processing.

Patients were identified from institutional databases and included if they had a confirmed diagnosis of OMG per MGFA clinical classification criteria and a minimum follow-up period of at least 6 months allowing assessment of conversion to generalized myasthenia gravis (GMG). Data were extracted using a standardised case-report template and subjected to internal consistency checks (e.g., unit harmonisation, range verification, and cross-validation of dates for disease-onset and follow-up). Where conflicting entries were identified, the electronic record was reviewed to adjudicate values; remaining missingness was retained for multiple imputation rather than excluded.

### Candidate Predictors and Outcome

Candidate predictors were selected a priori from routinely available clinical and laboratory variables captured at the time of OMG classification, yielding eight candidates in total: sex, onset age (continuous), onset symptom subtype, neostigmine test result, repetitive nerve stimulation (RNS) status, and thymic radiology findings. Follow-up time was included as a contextual covariate in the logistic model only to adjust for variable observation windows; we emphasize that it reflects the observation period and is not a baseline prediction variable. A constant "ThymusStatus" variable (reflecting surgical/histopathological status constant in this cohort due to referral patterns) was excluded from modelling due to lack of variability. Autoimmune comorbidities were excluded from the candidate set due to extreme imbalance (444 vs 10), which could compromise model stability and generalisability. The final modelling cohort comprised 454 patients.

The primary outcome was conversion from OMG to GMG during follow-up, defined as the development of any non-ocular MG symptoms (bulbar, limb, or respiratory).

### Missing Data Handling

To handle missing data among the remaining predictors, we performed multiple imputation using chained equations (MICE, m=10) with predictive mean matching. The imputation model included the outcome variable to preserve associations between predictors and outcome. The primary analysis was performed on the first imputed dataset; sensitivity analyses across all 10 imputed datasets yielded consistent results (data not shown).

### Model Development

**Logistic regression** was performed with stepwise selection based on the Akaike information criterion (AIC). Onset age was modelled as a continuous linear variable after evaluation of nonlinearity. Follow-up time was included as a covariate to adjust for variable observation windows, though it is not known at baseline and thus cannot serve as a clinical prediction variable at the time of diagnosis.

**Random forest (RF)** was trained with 300 trees, a maximum depth of 7, minimum samples per split of 10, and balanced class weights to address outcome imbalance. Variable importance was assessed using permutation-based importance scores.

**Gradient-boosted decision trees (XGBoost)** were implemented with a learning rate (eta) of 0.05 and maximum tree depth of 5, using 400 boosting rounds and a subsample ratio of 0.8. Hyperparameters were fixed based on preliminary evaluation; we acknowledge that more systematic tuning (e.g., grid or random search with cross-validation) could potentially alter XGBoost performance, and this represents a limitation in the fairness of model comparison.

### Internal Validation and Performance Assessment

Internal validation was performed using a single 70/30 training-test split, with bootstrap-resampled (500 iterations) AUC confidence intervals derived on the test set. We note that this approach differs from formal Efron-Gong bootstrap optimism correction; future work should adopt formal optimism correction or cross-validation. Discrimination was assessed with the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC) with 95% confidence intervals. Calibration was evaluated using calibration plots (loess-smoothed), calibration slope, calibration intercept, and Brier score. Clinical utility was summarised with decision-curve analysis (DCA) over threshold probabilities of 0.1–0.6. The events-per-variable ratio was 16.9 (135 events/8 variables), which is adequate for logistic regression but may be marginal for complex machine learning models with greater effective parameter counts.

All analyses were performed using R version 4.3.2 with the following packages: `mice` (3.16.0), `randomForest` (4.7-1.1), `xgboost` (1.7.5.1), `pROC` (1.18.0), `rms` (6.7-0), and `rmda` (1.6).

## Results

The study cohort comprised 454 patients with OMG, of whom 135 converted to GMG (29.7%). Converters were older at baseline (51.0 vs 39.6 years; p<0.001) and had shorter follow-up time prior to conversion assessment (15.7 ± 14.4 vs 30.0 ± 22.1 months). Key laboratory and imaging differences were also evident: RNS positivity was substantially more frequent among converters (73.5% vs 36.6%), and thymic radiology characteristics differed between groups (Table 1). The shorter follow-up among converters reflects that conversion events occurred early in the observation period — this is an informative censoring phenomenon rather than a baseline predictive signal.

### Logistic Regression

In multivariable logistic regression, four variables were retained after stepwise AIC selection: RNS status (OR 4.63, 95% CI 2.63–8.16), thymic radiology (OR 3.75, 95% CI 2.04–6.91), onset age (OR 1.02 per year, 95% CI 1.00–1.04), and follow-up time (OR 0.97 per month, 95% CI 0.96–0.99). We emphasize that follow-up time reflects the observation window and is not a baseline predictor — it was included to partially adjust for variable follow-up duration but introduces potential bias and limits clinical applicability at the time of prediction. The neostigmine test was not retained (p = 0.136 in stepwise selection) and did not reach significance in any model after adjustment for stronger predictors such as RNS and age. The logistic model demonstrated moderate discrimination with an AUC of 0.777 (95% CI 0.697–0.853), a Brier score of 0.183, and a calibration slope of 0.91 (indicating mild overfitting).

### Random Forest

The RF model yielded an AUC of 0.796 (95% CI 0.718–0.866), with a Brier score of 0.176 and calibration slope of 0.88. Feature-importance profiles indicated that follow-up time, onset age, and RNS status were the most influential variables (Figure 4), aligning with the directionality observed in the logistic model. However, the high importance of follow-up time (30.5% relative importance) partly reflects the temporal structure of conversion rather than a true baseline predictive signal, as patients who convert early have shorter observed follow-up. Decision-curve analysis suggested modest net benefit for RF over logistic regression at threshold probabilities of approximately 0.2–0.5 (Figures 2–3).

### XGBoost

XGBoost provided lower discrimination than RF, with an AUC of 0.708 (95% CI 0.601–0.796), a Brier score of 0.196, and a calibration slope of 0.72 (indicating more pronounced overfitting). The lower performance may partially reflect the absence of systematic hyperparameter tuning for this model, and head-to-head comparisons should be interpreted with this caveat.

### Model Comparison

The RF model showed a modestly higher AUC point estimate (0.796) compared with logistic regression (0.777) and XGBoost (0.708). However, the 95% confidence intervals for RF (0.718–0.866) and logistic regression (0.697–0.853) overlapped substantially, and no formal DeLong test was performed. The three models therefore demonstrated comparable discriminative performance (Table 3; Figures 1–3). Brier scores and calibration metrics likewise indicated no clear superiority of any single approach. Overall, these results indicate that while the RF model achieved a slightly higher point estimate, the differences among the three models were not statistically significant and should not be interpreted as demonstrating ML superiority over conventional regression.

## Discussion

In this study of 454 patients with ocular myasthenia gravis (OMG), we developed and internally validated three prediction models — logistic regression, random forest (RF), and extreme gradient boosting (XGBoost) — to identify patients at risk of conversion to generalized myasthenia gravis (GMG). The RF model showed a marginally higher AUC point estimate of 0.796 (95% CI 0.718–0.866) compared with logistic regression (AUC 0.777, 95% CI 0.697–0.853) and XGBoost (AUC 0.708, 95% CI 0.601–0.796), though confidence intervals overlapped substantially and the three models demonstrated comparable discriminative performance. Repetitive nerve stimulation (RNS) abnormality emerged as the most robust and stable predictor across all modeling approaches, with an odds ratio of 4.63 in the logistic regression model. These findings indicate that routinely available clinical variables can provide moderate discriminative ability for OMG-to-GMG conversion, but the incremental value of machine learning over conventional regression in this dataset is modest and not statistically significant.

Our results are broadly consistent with the existing OMG conversion prediction literature. The pooled proportion of 39% (95% CI 32%–47%; I² = 95.86%) reported in a recent meta-analysis of 31 studies by Fang et al. [PMID 37643888] encompasses a broad range of estimates; our observed conversion rate of 29.7% falls within this range, though toward the lower end, which may reflect our cohort's predominantly Asian composition and referral patterns. Several recent nomogram-based studies have reported comparable discriminative performance. Bi et al. [PMID 36469201] developed a dynamic nomogram in 435 patients with a C-index of 0.736, identifying RNS abnormality, thymoma, and AChR antibody positivity as key predictors. Ruan et al. [PMID 35874731] constructed a seven-center nomogram (501 patients) achieving C-indexes of 0.733 and 0.788 in development and validation cohorts, with RNS and thymic status selected alongside sex and AChR serostatus. Our logistic regression model (AUC 0.777) is situated within this performance range, confirming that routine clinical variables can yield practically useful discrimination.

When comparing with prior ML-based approaches, our RF model's point estimate (AUC 0.796) falls toward the upper end of reported ranges. Guéguen et al. [PMID 36001142] reported a C-statistic of 0.68 in their French cohort of 151 patients. Li et al. [PMID 35755967] externally validated a German-derived nomogram in a Chinese cohort and obtained a C-index of 0.670. However, direct comparison with these studies is limited by differences in validation strategies (internal vs external), variable sets (AChR antibody inclusion), and performance metrics (AUC vs C-index). The absence of a DeLong test and overlapping confidence intervals in our study preclude definitive claims about relative model superiority.

Several important divergences from prior studies merit discussion. Sex was not retained as a significant predictor in any of our three models, and the recent meta-analysis by Fang et al. [PMID 37643888] found a pooled risk ratio of only 1.06 (95% CI 0.96–1.17) for female sex, supporting the notion that sex is a weak and inconsistent predictor. The neostigmine test was a candidate predictor but did not reach significance in any model after adjustment for RNS and onset age, suggesting that in the presence of electrophysiological data, the bedside pharmacological test provides limited incremental value. The most conspicuous absence in our dataset is acetylcholine receptor (AChR) antibody serostatus, which has been among the most consistently identified predictors across cohorts — Bi et al. [PMID 36469201] reported an HR of 1.541 for AChR positivity, Ruan et al. [PMID 35874731] retained it in their final nomogram, and Li et al. [PMID 35755967] incorporated AChR antibody titer as a continuous variable. Direct comparison with AChR-inclusive models is precluded by differences in variable sets; our models cannot be said to "match" or "exceed" the performance of models that include AChR data. The absence of AChR antibody data is a significant limitation that restricts the clinical applicability of our models to settings where comprehensive serological profiling is available.

The mechanistic rationale for RNS as the dominant predictor warrants consideration. RNS positivity in OMG patients reflects subclinical neuromuscular junction (NMJ) dysfunction extending beyond the clinically affected ocular muscles. Kim et al. [PMID 33835748] demonstrated that RNS abnormalities in limb muscles (trapezius, abductor digiti minimi, flexor carpi ulnaris) conferred a hazard ratio of 5.19 for conversion, substantially higher than for facial nerve RNS. RNS positivity may thus serve as an electrophysiological biomarker of broader NMJ reserve depletion — a sign that the immunological attack has already compromised a sufficient proportion of postsynaptic acetylcholine receptors beyond the ocular muscles such that a decremental response becomes detectable. This interpretation is supported by our finding that RNS retained independent predictive value after controlling for thymic status and onset age, and moreover that it was the most stable predictor across all three modeling frameworks.

The clinical implications of our findings require cautious interpretation. RNS testing is a simple, widely available, and inexpensive neurophysiological investigation that can be performed in most neurology departments. Our results suggest that OMG patients with a positive RNS study at initial evaluation — particularly those with additional risk factors such as thymic abnormalities or older onset age — may be at elevated risk for conversion and could potentially benefit from early immunotherapy, an approach supported by meta-analytic evidence [PMID 37643888]. However, follow-up time is not a baseline predictor, which limits the logistic model's applicability at the point of first diagnosis. The logistic regression model, with three clinically available baseline predictors (RNS, thymic radiology, onset age) plus follow-up time as a contextual covariate, requires external validation before consideration for any clinical application.

### Limitations

**Limitation 1: Single-center retrospective design and validation limitations.** This is the most important limitation of the study. Although we employed standardized data extraction and MICE for missing data, internal validation cannot substitute for external validation, and performance estimates from internal validation are often optimistic. The events-per-variable ratio of 16.9 is adequate for logistic regression but may be marginal for complex ML models. Furthermore, the validation strategy (single 70/30 split with bootstrap-resampled AUC CIs) differs from formal bootstrap optimism correction. These models require independent external validation in diverse populations before any clinical application. The absence of external validation is the most important limitation — these models require independent validation before any clinical application.

**Limitation 2: Absence of AChR antibody data and incomplete serological profiling.** AChR antibody seropositivity and titer have been among the most reproducible predictors of OMG conversion in prior nomogram studies [PMID 36469201, PMID 35874731, PMID 35755967], and their unavailability may have reduced the upper bound of discriminative performance. Direct comparison with AChR-inclusive models is precluded. The absence of these data limits the model's applicability in settings where antibody testing is routinely available. Future prospective studies incorporating comprehensive autoantibody profiling (AChR, MuSK, LRP4, agrin) and standardized titer quantification will enable more complete risk stratification.

**Limitation 3: Binary outcome classification with temporal confounding.** Modeling OMG-to-GMG conversion as a binary endpoint precludes estimation of conversion risk at specific time intervals. Follow-up time was included as a covariate to partially adjust for variable observation windows, but this introduces a methodological problem — follow-up time is not known at baseline and should not be treated as a baseline predictor. Including it partially adjusts for time-related bias but limits clinical applicability at the time of first diagnosis. Future work should adopt Cox proportional hazards or competing-risk frameworks (e.g., Fine-Gray models) to generate time-dependent risk predictions and should develop a model using only baseline-available variables.

**Limitation 4: Missing RNS data and protocol heterogeneity.** The 19% missing rate for RNS data highlights that RNS testing was not performed uniformly in this retrospective cohort. MICE addresses missingness under the missing-at-random assumption, which is unverifiable in this dataset. Additionally, data collection spanned several years during which RNS testing protocols may have varied, potentially introducing non-differential misclassification. Sensitivity analyses examining extreme missing-data scenarios and standardized prospective RNS protocols are needed to strengthen the precision of future prediction models.

## Conclusion

This study demonstrates that logistic regression, random forest, and XGBoost models using routinely available clinical variables can predict OMG-to-GMG conversion with moderate, comparable discriminative performance (AUC 0.71–0.80). RF showed a marginally higher AUC point estimate (0.796) than logistic regression (0.777), but confidence intervals overlapped substantially, and no statistically significant difference was established. RNS abnormality was the single most informative predictor across all modeling frameworks. However, follow-up time — a variable not available at baseline — was included in the logistic model, limiting its point-of-care applicability. The absence of AChR antibody data, the reliance on internal rather than external validation, and the modest sample size for complex ML models together constrain the generalizability of these findings. The most important limitation is the absence of external validation — these models require independent validation in diverse populations before any clinical application. Future work should prioritize (1) prospective multicenter data collection with comprehensive serological profiling, (2) time-to-event analytic frameworks, (3) a baseline-only prediction model without follow-up time, and (4) external validation to establish transportability before clinical deployment.
